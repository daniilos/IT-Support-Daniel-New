There's one phrase my manager repeats more than any other.

"Just ask ChatGPT."

And this hasn't been about IT for a long time. Not about scripts, not about configuration, not about diagnostics. It's about everything: documents, copy, ads, answers, tasks, ideas, letters, posts. If something's unclear — ChatGPT. If something isn't selling — rewrite it in ChatGPT. If you need to think — also ChatGPT.

At some point it starts sounding almost comical. You're sitting right there, working, and every five minutes you hear: "put that in GPT" — "no, I'll ask GPT myself" — "GPT will phrase it better" — "let's see what GPT says first."

And then you catch yourself with an uncomfortable thought: if everything goes through GPT, why do they still need someone who was hired as a specialist?

---

> **Short version:** ChatGPT dramatically lowers the barrier — for writing, searching, phrasing. But a good-sounding answer isn't the same as the right decision. GPT doesn't know your processes. GPT takes no responsibility. And GPT can't tell what does or doesn't apply in your specific situation. The specialist's role doesn't disappear — but it changes. You're no longer the person with the answers. You're the person who filters, judges, and takes responsibility.

---

## The barrier really has dropped

To be fair: GPT does impressive things.

It writes fast, searches fast, phrases things fast, and gives people the feeling they already know what they're talking about. For managers and business owners that looks like magic: why spend time consulting an expert when you can get a clear answer in a chat in 20 seconds?

But a clear answer isn't a good answer.

GPT doesn't live inside your business. It doesn't know the weak spots, doesn't know what broke before, doesn't understand what your team can and can't do. It helps brilliantly with writing, structuring, and exploring. But it doesn't replace mature professional judgement — and it's not hiding that: it just always gives an answer, even when it doesn't know.

## The specialist becomes an interface

This is the part a lot of specialists recognise now.

You're hired as someone with experience. But then you're told: "do it this way, because GPT said so" or "I preferred the GPT version" or "can you just adapt what it wrote?"

Formally you're still the expert. But in practice you sometimes become an interface — not the one who decides, but the one who finishes the result someone else already chose.

Before, a specialist thought, analysed and proposed a solution. Now what's increasingly expected isn't expertise but confirmation. Not "what's better for the business?" but "GPT phrased it like this, can you finish it off?"

(I'm exaggerating slightly. But not much.)

## GPT isn't the problem

The problem isn't that ChatGPT exists. I use it every day myself. The problem is the overestimation of what it actually does.

GPT lowers the barrier for generating answers. That's not the same as lowering the barrier for making good decisions. Rephrasing a text or generating an idea now takes five seconds. But judging whether that idea makes sense in your specific context — that still takes experience.

And that's exactly where the tension sits. People start thinking: play around with AI for a couple of weeks and you're already reasoning at nearly specialist level. That's true for phrasing. It's not true for judging.

## What's expected of a specialist now

The role is changing. That's just the reality.

Before, you were the person with the answers. That's no longer enough on its own — because GPT also has answers, and they're always fast and always confident.

What remains — and what GPT can't do — is this: knowing when the answer is right, when it's superficial, and when it's dangerous to just go along with it. Filtering, interpreting, and taking responsibility for the outcome.

That's less spectacular than before. But it's what matters now.

So yes, "just ask ChatGPT." I do it too. But after that, there's still someone needed who figures out whether the answer actually holds up — or whether they're just efficiently executing a nice-looking mistake.

## Three questions I get asked

**Should a small business use ChatGPT for IT decisions?**
For phrasing questions, finding information or summarising documentation: yes, genuinely useful. For making technical decisions about your specific infrastructure: not without someone who knows the context. GPT doesn't know what went wrong at your place last week.

**Is my IT person still relevant when AI can do so much?**
More than before, precisely because AI can produce so much. The question is no longer "who has the answer" — GPT has that too. The question is "who judges whether the answer is right for this specific situation". That's what a specialist with real experience does.

**How do I use ChatGPT smartly in my office without blindly trusting it?**
Use it for the first draft, the quick summary, the phrasing. Never let it have the final say on anything that affects your data, your security or your clients. And if you're unsure — ask someone who understands the consequences, not just the answer.
